Equality Versus Inequality

The difference between equality and inequality is quite simple. Equality is the state of being equal or balanced. Inequality is the state of being unequal or unbalanced.

Inequality has been going on for years in the government. One of the many examples being the right to vote for women (The Women's Suffrage Movement.)

Solving the Formula

The inequality that we face in our governments are the same as the ones we face in our math problems.

Just like in math, the way to solve an inequality is by simplifying the inequality until you are left with a variable on its own.

So based off this knowledge, the new question is: How can we solve problems a problem like inequality?

One of the more complicated or specific answer is increase the minimum wage, expand the Earned Income Tax, build assets for working families, invest in education, make the tax code more progressive, and end residential segregation.

The Women's Suffrage Movement

What Was The Women's Suffrage Movement?

The Women's Suffrage Movement was a fight for the right to vote. At the time, many opposed giving women the privilege to vote for a couple of reasons. Some worried about the anticorruption efforts of women. Others worried that women voters would support minimum wage and child labor laws. Most actually beleived that women were supposed to stay as homemakers and mothers and not politically active citizens. Nevertheless, women still fought and soon began to gain national support by the 1890s.

Who Are Some Women That Were Involved?

Susan B. Anthony

A Little Bit About Her

Susan Brownell Anthony was born on February 15, 1820, in Adams, Massachusetts. Susan B. Anthony was the daughter of Daniel and Lucy Anthony. She grew up in a politically active family who worked to end slavery. Susan B. Anthony died on March 13, 1906 at the age of 86.

Her Role in the Movement

Susan B. Anthony was a pioneer in the women’s suffrage movement in the United States and president of the National American Woman Suffrage Association.

Elizabeth Cady Stanton

A Little Bit About Her

Elizabeth Cady Stanton was born on November 12, 1815 in Johnstown, New York. Elizabeth Cady Stanton was the daughter of Daniel Cady and Margaret Livingston. Elizabeth’s father was a slave owner, prominent attorney, Congressman and judge who exposed his daughter to the study of law and other so-called male domains early in her life. Elizabeth Cady Stanton died on October 26, 1902 at the age of 86.

Her Role in the Movement

Elizabeth Cady Stanton was an abolitionist, human rights activist and one of the first leaders of the woman’s rights movement.

Alice Paul

A Little Bit About Her

Alice Paul was born on January 11, 1885 in Moorestown, New Jersey. Alice Paul was the daughter of Tacie and William Paul. Alice Paul was born into a Quaker family which meant she was raised in an intellectual and religious environment. Alice Paul died on July 9, 1977 at the age of 92.

Her Role in the Movement

While attending a training school in England, Alice Paul became active with the country’s radical suffragists. After two years with the National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA), she cofounded the Congressional Union and then formed the National Woman’s party in 1916.

Lucy Stone

A Little Bit About Her

Lucy Stone was born on August 1818 in West Brookfield, Massachusetts. Lucy Stone was the daughter of Hannah and Francis Stone. Lucy Stone's parents were committed abolitionists who believed in fighting against slavery. Lucy Stone died on October 18, 1893 at he age of 75.

Her Role in the Movement

Lucy Stone was an orator and advocate who fought for women's rights.

Ida B. Wells

A Little Bit About Her

Ida Bell Wells was born on July 16,1862 in Holly Springs, Mississippi. Ida B. Wells was the daughter of James and Lizzie Wells. During Reconstruction, Ida B. Wells' parents were active in the Republican Party. Ida B. Wells died on March 25, 1931 at the age of 68.

Her Role in the Movement

Ida B. Wells was an African-American journalist and activist who fought in the woman suffrage movement.

Impact

In 1919, the U.S. Congress passed the Nineteeth Amendment, granting American woman the right to vote. The Nineteeth Amendment was ratified by the states the following year, making it a law.The success represented years of effort and marked a dramatic shift in American democracy.

The result also led to higher expectations for women and an increase in economic roles in society for women.

Some Interesting Facts

White Versus Black

Although women of diverse backgrounds fought for suffrage, the movement’s leadership remained white and middle class. Black women in particular were forced to fight on the margins and faced racism from suffragists who saw black activism as a direct threat to the cause.

Different Opinions

Not all women wanted the vote, and many vehemently fought against suffrage.

Adding to the Arguement

Women fought for more than just the right to vote. They also fought for legal protections, greater employment oppurtunities, and finacial independence.

WSM Timeline

1848:The first convention is held at Seneca Falls.

1850:The convention moves to Worcester gaining one thousand plus participants.

1868: The 14th Amendment is ratified.

1869: The movement is split between Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton.

1870: The 15th Amendment is ratified.

1872: Susan B. Anthony us arrested.

1878: The WSM is first introduced to Congress.

1890: The National Women's Suffrage Association and The American Women's Suffrage Association merge forming the National American Women Suffrage Association (NAWSA).

1893: Colorado is the first to accept the amendment allowing women the right to vote.

1896: The National Association of Colored Women is formed.

1913: The Congressional Union for Women Suffrage is formed by Alice Paul and Lucy Burns.

1916: The National Women's Party (NWP) is formed by Alice Paul and her colleagues.

1917: Alice Paul and other July protesters were arrested for obstructing traffic.

1818: President Wilson announces women's suffrage was needed as a "war measure".

1819: The Federal Woman Suffrage Amendment was passed by the House of Representatives and Senate and is sent to the state for ratification.

August 26, 1920: The 19th Amendment of the Constitution is signed into a law granting women the right to finally vote.
